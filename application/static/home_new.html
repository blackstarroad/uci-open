Spotlight on UCI Open Education

This week, UCI OpenCourseWare presents Open Education Week featuring Professor Michael Dennin and his views on Open Educational Resources.

Why it's important: Professor Michael Dennin explains that because UCI is a public University, it is essential that it provides its educational resources not only within its local bounds but across a broad spectrum. He explains that this can be done through the use of educational videos as students will be able to learn from the comfort of their homes or any place that they may be. Furthermore, the use of videos benefits students by allowing them to replay and relearn anything that they may have missed or could not comprehend the first time. Whether they are a UCI student who participates in an in-person lecture for that course or a student from the general public who uses the provided videos as a prime medium for their learning, open education will address the needs of all those with the desire to learn.

Professor Dennin conveys that a wide range of science courses are provided to the general public through Open Education such as a variety of physics videos and a number of chemistry videos from our OpenChem courses. However, it is not limited to just science courses, a multitude of courses from different subjects are provided at the disposal of all those in need of them. These courses can be taken in any order and there are no prerequisites required for any single course, so no matter what level you are at or what field you are experienced in you can take any course you want any time you want.

Michael Dennin is a professor of Physics in the School of Physical Sciences and has provided a variety of educational resources to the public through his online courses such as Physics 3A: Basic Physics and Physics 50: Math Methods

Keyword search and filtered search

After entering a term in the search field in the upper right side of any page, you will get a results screen. Here you will be provided the opportunity to further filter your results, including by type of resource (video, course, etc...) or by school and department.
